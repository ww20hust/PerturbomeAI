Introduction
Genetic variants are naturally occurring perturbations of biological systems. By altering molecular pathways, cellular functions, and physiological processes, they generate the phenotypic diversity observed across individuals—from subtle shifts in quantitative traits to overt disease. Understanding how these perturbations propagate from genotype to phenotype remains a central challenge in biology and medicine.Current approaches to interpreting variant effects operate predominantly in a genotype-to-phenotype direction. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) link genetic loci to individual traits or diseases, while polygenic risk scores (PRS) aggregate effects across many variants to estimate overall genetic liability for specific phenotypes. At the level of individual variants, tools such as AlphaMissense leverage protein structure and evolutionary conservation to predict missense variant pathogenicity, and AlphaGenome predicts regulatory consequences of both coding and non-coding variants from DNA sequence alone. These approaches have transformed human genetics, yet they share fundamental limitations: GWAS and PRS capture associations with one phenotype at a time and provide limited insight into the multivariate physiological consequences of perturbation at a given locus; sequence-based predictors yield per-variant scores that do not account for the individual-level phenotypic context in which a variant operates. If phenotypes are shaped by genetic perturbations, a natural complementary question arises: can the effects of genetic perturbation be inferred directly from the phenotypic signatures they produce (Figure 1A)?Answering this question is non-trivial. The phenotypic consequences of a genetic perturbation are typically distributed across multiple physiological systems rather than confined to a single measurement. A variant affecting lipid metabolism, for example, may simultaneously alter hepatic enzymes, inflammatory markers, glucose homeostasis, and hematological parameters. These coordinated, multi-system signatures are often subtle, high-dimensional, and entangled with environmental and stochastic variation—making them effectively invisible to unaided clinical reasoning and inaccessible to conventional single-trait analytical frameworks.However, recent work in machine learning has demonstrated that latent biological states can be recovered from routine measurements not originally collected for that purpose. Deep learning models infer biological age from standard clinical laboratory panels, predict spatial transcriptomic profiles from routine hematoxylin-and-eosin-stained histology images, and reconstruct cardiac structure from electrocardiograms. These successes establish a general principle: biological perturbations leave distributed signatures across observable data, and sufficiently expressive models can decode them. We hypothesized that genetic perturbations similarly imprint detectable, locus-specific signatures within routine clinical phenotypes—and that these signatures can be systematically extracted.
Here we introduce PerturbomeAI, a scalable phenotype-to-gene reverse estimation framework that systematically decodes locus-specific genetic perturbations directly from routine clinical measurements (Strategy #3 in Figure 1A). Departing from standard forward genetic approaches like single-trait GWAS or polygenic risk scores (Strategy #1 and #2 in Figure 1A), PerturbomeAI decodes the distributed, multi-system phenotypic imprints left by individual genetic variations across the human genome. For each targeted locus, the framework stratifies individuals into binary training labels based strictly on their factual genotype as carrier versus non-carrier status (Figure 1B). Trained on population-scale biobank data with paired phenotypes and genotypes, PerturbomeAI translates high-dimensional phenotypic signals into an individual-level Genetic Perturbation Score (GPS) (Figure 1C). The GPS functions as a continuous, quantitative measure of the degree to which an individual's clinical phenotype mirrors the downstream biological consequences of a specific genetic perturbation. We demonstrate the robust generalizability of these models through rigorous multi-cohort evaluations, achieving consistent discriminative performance and odds ratio separation across internal test sets and independent clinical validation cohorts (Figure 1D).
By reconstructing these latent physiological states, PerturbomeAI establishes a versatile synthetic phenotype that unlocks transformative workflows across functional genomics, gene discovery, and precision medicine (Figure 1E–H). For a given locus-specific model, evaluating the GPS across carriers of other genetic loci reveals whether disparate variants perturb the same underlying biological pathway, as well as their functional directionality; this enables the direct, disease-label-free functional interpretation of rare variants and variants of uncertain significance (VUS) (Figure 1E). Furthermore, because the GPS represents a genotype-guided synthetic phenotype, it can be deployed directly as a trait in downstream genome-wide association studies (GWAS) to uncover novel variants and genetic modifiers operating within the same pathway (Figure 1F). Critically, PerturbomeAI functions as a virtual genetic test for unsequenced phenomic cohorts, such as pure proteomics datasets, allowing researchers to impute genetic perturbation states and characterize deep multi-omic signatures without direct DNA sequencing (Figure 1G). Even within sequenced multi-omic cohorts where standard rare-variant analyses are severely underpowered due to the extreme scarcity of factual carriers, the GPS provides a continuous metric for every single individual in the population, enabling robust, fully powered regression modeling. Finally, on an individual patient level, computing the GPS from low-cost, routine clinical panels identifies individuals exhibiting extreme carrier-like physiology, establishing a highly efficient screening tier to guide targeted sequencing for high-risk variants, such as the JAK2 V617F mutation or APOE risk alleles. Moreover, GPS can distinguish genetically driven from non-genetic disease subtypes, thereby informing therapeutic stratification and more precise clinical management (Figure 1H). Together, these diverse applications demonstrate that routine clinical phenotypes harbor a rich, accessible reservoir of genomic insights, offering a powerful blueprint for scalable precision medicine.
 
Theoretical Framework and Simulation Model of PerturbomeAI
 
To establish the theoretical foundations and statistical conditions under which phenotype-to-genotype reverse inference is computationally feasible, we first evaluated the PerturbomeAI framework using a forward generative simulation model. We simulated two continuous clinical traits (Y1, Y2) arising from twenty independent, biallelic genetic loci (G1 to G20) in a synthetic cohort of 50,000 individuals. To simulate the human genome architecture, in which minor allele frequencies (MAF) were distributed across a log-uniform grid ranging from highly rare to common (Figure 2A). Locus effect sizes were inversely coupled to their frequency via a negative selection power-law model, mirroring the empirical frequency-dependent genetic architecture of complex human traits. Genotypes were sampled independently, and multivariate Gaussian noise was introduced to simulate environmental and stochastic variance. This design established a transparent ground truth where frequency and penetrance were explicitly coupled yet could be independently manipulated.
 
XGBoost classifiers were trained to decode carrier status (heterozygous or homozygous) at each independent locus based on the simulated two-dimensional phenotypes. Using an 8-fold cross-validation scheme, we observed a strict, monotonic decline in predictive performance as a function of increasing allele frequency and decreasing effect size, with the test Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (AUC-ROC) dropping from 0.97 for rare variants down to 0.61 for common variants (Figure 2B). To gain geometric intuition into this performance gradient, we visualized the individuals within the two-dimensional phenotype space (Figure S2). Carriers of rare, large-effect alleles formed tight, compact clusters distinctively displaced from the population center along a well-defined direction of effect. Conversely, carriers of common, weak-effect alleles were highly numerous but phenotypically dispersed, demonstrating massive structural overlap with non-carriers. This phenomenon reflects a substantially higher per-carrier signal-to-noise ratio and lower phenotypic heterogeneity among rare-variant carriers, demonstrating that negative selection preserves high phenotypic penetrance at low population frequencies. We further conducted a causal separation experiment by testing scenarios of "constant effect with varying frequencies" (Figure 2C, left) and "constant frequency with varying effects" (Figure 2C, right) and established that the predictive advantage of rare variants fundamentally stems from their larger effect sizes, rather than their low frequency per se.
 
We next systematically analyzed how the training sample size and carrier representation modulate model performance across distinct frequency spectra via downsampling experiments (Figure 2D). For a representative rare locus (G7), the test AUC-ROC rose sharply and reached a high performance plateau with minimal sample sizes, demonstrating that sample size for rare variants primarily serves to guarantee basic carrier representation before the strong phenotypic signal saturates model capacity. In stark contrast, for a representative common locus (G17), the test AUC-ROC improved at an exceptionally gradual rate, asymptoting modestly above chance even when the training set contained thousands of carriers. This behavior indicates that common variant decoding is constrained by an intrinsic ceiling set by a low signal-to-noise ratio, which cannot be overcome simply by increasing sample size. Collectively, these simulations establish that rare variants imprint sharp, distinct physiological signatures that are fully recoverable with limited carrier representation, whereas common variants leave faint, highly diffused phenotypic traces that large sample sizes cannot completely resolve.
 
Development and Systematic Validation of PerturbomeAI Across Real-World Population-Scale Biobanks
 
Having validated the theoretical feasibility of phenotype-to-genotype reverse inference, we next translated the PerturbomeAI framework to real-world, population-scale human data. We utilized routinely collected clinical laboratory panels as our high-dimensional phenotypic input to predict individual genotypes. To minimize potential confounding effects from population stratification and ethnically induced genetic bias, initial model development and optimization were restricted to a discovery cohort comprising approximately 400,000 Caucasian participants from the UK Biobank (UKB) with fully paired, high-depth phenotypic and genomic data.
 
We systematically curated four independent genetic variant source sets to undergo GPS modeling: (1) Exonic variants, (2) Non-coding regulatory variants, (3) Pharmacogenomics-associated variants, and (4) Rare variants aggregated for gene-level collapsing effect (Figure S1). We first established variant-level modeling for individual loci across the first three datasets. For each specific variant, individuals were stratified into a binary classification schema: positive labels were assigned to carriers (homozygous or heterozygous for the alternative allele), and negative labels were assigned to non-carriers (homozygous for the reference allele). To manage the immense computational demands of modeling hundreds of thousands of individual loci across half a million individuals, we engineered a highly parallelized pipeline. The total cohort was partitioned into an 8-fold cross-validation architecture, and training operations were distributed across 8 GPUs, ensuring rigorous out-of-fold cross-validated scoring for every single individual. To systematically evaluate the discriminative performance, robustness, and biological utility of each trained model, we quantified the out-of-fold GPS distributions between true carriers and non-carriers using three rigorous statistical metrics: Cohen’s d effect size, the median score difference (Delta GPS median = GPS carriers - GPS non-carriers), and false discovery rate (FDR)-adjusted p-values. We established a strict primary threshold of Delta GPS median > 0.1 between carriers and non-carriers to define a biologically informative and successful model, indicating that routine clinical laboratory phenotypes contain sufficient physiological signal to robustly decode the locus-specific perturbation. To guarantee basic statistical power and prevent models from overfitting to stochastic noise, variant-level modeling was strictly limited to loci possessing at least two carriers and two non-carriers within each fold.
 
Within the exonic variant dataset, 313,752 variants satisfied the carrier threshold criteria and underwent a formal GPS modeling. Among these, 32,391 variants demonstrated statistically significant differences in out-of-fold GPS distributions between carriers and non-carriers (FDR-adjusted p <= 0.05), and 3,001 variants yielded successful models with a Delta GPS median > 0.1 (Figure 2E, Figure S1B). For the non-coding regulatory variant dataset, 36,845 variants successfully generated completed GPS models. Among this modeled set, 28,753 variants exhibited significant carrier versus non-carrier separation (FDR-adjusted p <= 0.05), with 40 variants yielding successful models with a Delta GPS median > 0.1 (Figure 2E, Figure S1C). In the pharmacogenomics variant category, 4,734 variants successfully underwent GPS modeling. Of these, 3,944 variants demonstrated statistically significant separation between carriers and non-carriers (FDR-adjusted p <= 0.05), and 47 variants yielded successful models with a Delta GPS median > 0.1 (Figure 2E, Figure S1D). Finally, at the gene-level collapsing scale, 7,575 genes were successfully modeled. Among these, 467 genes demonstrated highly significant aggregate phenotypic signatures (FDR-adjusted p <= 0.05), with 101 genes yielding successful models with a Delta GPS median > 0.1 (Figure 2F).
 
To investigate the relationship between real-world model performance and the genetic architecture, we stratified the modeled exonic loci into distinct bins based on their Minor Allele Frequency (MAF). We observed that a higher proportion of variants within the lower frequency intervals yielded successful models with a Delta GPS median > 0.1, with this ratio expanding dramatically at the ultra-rare end of the spectrum (Figure 2G). This empirical finding strongly aligns with our theoretical forward simulation model; because rare variants are more likely to possess larger biological effect sizes, they exert a disproportionately larger, more cohesive effect on routine clinical phenotypes, rendering these specific loci inherently more amenable to successful model reconstruction. To validate our simulation's insights regarding training sample size in a real-world setting, we performed an empirical downsampling experiment on the successful JAK2 V617F model. Mirroring our theoretical predictions, systematic reduction of the training sample size demonstrated that the model’s discriminative capacity remained exceptionally stable and robust across a wide downsampling range, collapsing precipitously only when carrier representation was depleted past a critical threshold (Figure 2H).
 
We next examined the intra-genic behavior of variants by focusing on cases where multiple independent SNPs were successfully modeled within the same gene. Within-gene variant pairs frequently displayed elevated absolute SCC values, demonstrating that distinct mutations within the same locus converge on highly coordinated physiological signatures (Figure S3A). Clustering revealed that for genes such as ALPL, GPT, and SLC22A12, intra-genic SNPs cluster tightly together with positive correlations approaching 1, indicating shared downstream phenotypic manifestations across population-scale clinical measurements (Figure S3B and Figure S4). Intriguingly, we also identified instances where within-gene variant pairs exhibited strongly negative SCC values approaching -1 and separated into distinct branches during clustering. This pattern is prominent in genes like APOB (Figure S3A and Figure S3B), where a subset of variants demonstrates strong negative correlation against others within the same locus. This architecture highlights key functional divergence within a single gene, where specific alleles imprint highly overlapping but precisely opposing phenotypic trajectories, potentially distinguishing gain-of-function from loss-of-function variations. Conversely, certain genes exhibited an entirely decentralized intra-genic architecture; for example, successfully modeled variants within MUC4 maintained pairwise SCC values hovering near 0 and were widely dispersed across the global cluster (Figure S3A and Figure S4). This indicates that while multiple independent perturbations across MUC4 exert robust, detectable phenotypic effects, they disrupt highly distinct clinical sub-phenotypes and operate via independent physiological pathways rather than converging on a singular signature.
 
Beyond intra-genic convergence, we observed some inter-gene pairs also characterized by exceptionally high absolute pairwise SCC values among independent loci, mapping shared distributed phenotypic profiles across the genome (Figure S3C). High-scoring inter-gene pairs displayed near-perfect correlation structures across the population, capturing core biological pathways. For instance, the strong positive correlation observed between ALPL and GPLD1 aligns with their shared involvement in the post-translational processing and release of anchor-bound proteins, where GPLD1 directly modulates GPI-anchored alkaline phosphatase efficiency. Similarly, the highly correlated inter-gene relationship between PCSK9 and LDLR reflects their tightly coupled, direct physical interactome that dictates receptor degradation and receptor-mediated low-density lipoprotein clearance. These tight multi-genic alignments demonstrate that PerturbomeAI can map functional biological networks and discover epistatic or pathway-level relationships directly from routine clinical phenotypes.

Figure 1. Conceptual Framework and Downstream Applications of PerturbomeAI. (A) Analytical strategies to connect phenotypes to genes. Comparison of forward genetic mapping strategies and the reverse estimation strategy. Strategy 1 represents traditional Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS) linking a single genetic variant to a single trait. Strategy 2 outlines Polygenic Risk Scores (PRS) aggregating multiple genetic variants to predict a continuous trait or clinical risk. Strategy 3 represents the PerturbomeAI framework, a reverse estimation paradigm that leverages high-dimensional, multi-system phenotypic modalities, such as routine laboratory tests, optical coherence tomography (OCT) imaging, proteomics, and transcriptional regulation data, through advanced machine learning architectures to simultaneously infer multiple locus-specific genetic perturbations. (B) Workflow for label construction. Schematic illustration of individual-level label assignment within the biobank cohort. High-dimensional routine clinical laboratory features (including glycated hemoglobin, low-density lipoprotein cholesterol, alanine aminotransferase, and creatinine) are utilized to train an artificial intelligence predictor module. Performance is benchmarked using out-of-fold GPS distribution differences between carrier groups. (E) Evaluation paradigm for variant effect estimation.
